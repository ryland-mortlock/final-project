Problem statement¶

The question I'm interested in is this: do the two most frequently used individual defensive statistics, steals and blocks, actually correlate with good defensive ability.

The gold standard for defensive ability I will be using is the defensive rating (DRTG). It is very complicated to calculate and it estimates how many points a player allows per 100 posessions they are on the floor. A lower DRTG means a better defender.

Let's take a look at the distribution of DRTG in the dataset.

In [31]:
nba_df.DRTG.plot.hist(bins = 30);

It seems to be distributed mostly between 100 and 110 which makes sense considering the league average was 110 points per 100 posessions in the 2018-2019 season.

As a gut check for the DRTG statistic, let's make sure the Defensive Player of the Year from 2018-2019 season has a good DRTG.

It seems so. The outlying very good defenders and very bad defenders mostly play less than 15 minutes per game. To look at defensive statistics, we will only consider players who played more than 20 minutes per game.

nba_df_sub = nba_df[nba_df.MPG > 20]

Out[26]:
'Retaining 287 players or 46.14% of players.'

It may seem strange to a data scientist to throw out more than half of your data. However, a typical NBA team has an 8-man rotation of players that rotate in and out. So for a league of 30 teams, we are looking for a dataset of approximatelt 240 players to focus on those who play consistent minutes.

Do steals and blocks correlate with DRTG?¶

I am going to use the seaborn module to create nice plots and I will use the scipy stats module to create a function to calculate R-squared to display on the plot.

In [59]:
import seaborn as sns
from scipy import stats
def r2(x, y):
    return stats.pearsonr(x, y)[0] ** 2
In [60]:
sns.jointplot(x=nba_df_sub.SPG, y=nba_df_sub.DRTG, kind = "reg", stat_func=r2);

It does not look like there is any correlation between steals per game and defensive rating.

In [61]:
sns.jointplot(x=nba_df_sub.BPG, y=nba_df_sub.DRTG, kind = "reg", stat_func=r2);

Very interesting! Blocks per game do have some correlation with defensive rating. It does look like the correlation is driven by a small number of players with high blocks per game. The distribution of the BPG statistic makes me think that the defensive statistic might be position biased.

It looks like there is a clear bias in the defensive rating towards forwards and centers. Based on the distribution, I will consider 3 groups:

  1. Guard and Guard/Forwards
  2. Forwards
  3. Forward/Centers and Centers
